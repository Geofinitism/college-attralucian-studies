## 1. The Question Behind the Question

"Why" is everywhere in science and philosophy. Why does mass attract mass? Why is there something rather than nothing? Why does evolution produce complexity? These questions drive inquiry — they motivate theory construction, energise research programmes, and give explanation its sense of depth.

But when has "why" been examined in relation to *the conditions under which symbols themselves are formed?*

This essay performs that examination. It finds a **structural limit** — not a limit of knowledge or intelligence, but a limit built into the architecture of symbolic systems.

---

## 2. The Two Domains

Finite Symbolic Mechanics (FSM) begins with a commitment: **measurement is primary**. Symbols are not arbitrary abstractions; they arise from finite, resolution-bounded interactions. Every symbol requires:

- A **bounded capture** of interaction — the interaction must be registered within finite sensitivity limits
- A **minimum cost of distinction** ΔM — below this cost, two states cannot be distinguished
- **Compression** into a finite symbolic domain — the full interaction cannot be preserved; only what fits within the Alphonic container

This produces two domains that must be kept clearly distinct:

| Domain | Symbol | Character | Examples |
|--------|--------|-----------|---------|
| **Interaction domain** | ℐ | Inadmissible — cannot be directly represented | Raw physical processes; pre-measurement reality |
| **Symbolic domain** | S̃ | Admissible — all scientific work happens here | Equations, theorems, data, models, theories |

The tilde on S̃ marks it as a Geofinite construction — finite, bounded, measurement-grounded — not a classical mathematical space.

**All scientific models, all theorems, all explanations operate within S̃.**

---

## 3. The Generonic Projection

Symbol formation is the act of moving from ℐ to S̃. This is accomplished by the **generonic projection**:

$$\tilde{\mathcal{G}} : \mathcal{I} \rightarrow \tilde{\mathcal{S}}$$

G̃ is a **Generon** — a finite state machine that takes an interaction event as input and produces a stable symbolic form as output.

The crucial property: **G̃ is non-invertible.**

When the Generon captures an interaction and produces a symbol, information that falls below the resolution threshold ΔM is lost. That lost information cannot be recovered from the symbol alone. There is no function G̃⁻¹: S̃ → ℐ that recovers the full original interaction.

This is not a contingent failure of measurement technology. It is a structural consequence of operating within a finite symbolic system.

---

## 4. The Generonic Boundary

**Definition:** The Generonic Boundary is the interface at which interaction is projected into symbolic form under finite constraint.

It separates:
- ℐ (the inadmissible interaction domain — prior to symbol formation)
- S̃ (the admissible symbolic domain — where all representation lives)

**There exists no admissible operation that maps from S̃ back to ℐ.**

The boundary is a one-way gate. Interaction flows in, symbols come out. Symbols cannot flow back to recover the interaction that produced them.

This is the central structural fact of the essay. Everything else follows from it.

---

## 5. The Structure of "Why"

To ask "why does X occur?" is to request a **generative account** of X — a account that shows where X comes from, what produced it, what its origin is.

Formally, a generative "why" requires finding ℐ_X ∈ ℐ such that:

$$\tilde{\mathcal{G}}(\mathcal{I}_X) = \tilde{X}$$

That is: find the interaction that, when projected through the Generonic Boundary, produces X̃.

But **ℐ_X is not accessible within S̃**. The only things accessible in S̃ are symbols — the outputs of G̃, not its inputs.

Any admissible answer to a "why" question must therefore take the form:

$$\tilde{X} \sim f(\tilde{Y}_1,\; \tilde{Y}_2,\; \ldots)$$

A relation between symbols within S̃ — not a route back to ℐ.

---

## 6. The Bifurcation of "Why"

Every "why" question faces a fork:

```
"Why does X̃ occur?"
          │
          ▼
   What is being
     requested?
   ┌──────┴───────┐
   │              │
Relational    Generative
   │              │
X̃ ~ f(Ỹ₁...)  requires ℐ_X
Stays in S̃    Exits S̃
   │              │
ADMISSIBLE    INADMISSIBLE
```

**Relational "why"** — *"Why does F = ma?"* → *"Because F = ma is a stable relation within classical mechanics, consistent with observations Ỹ₁, Ỹ₂, ... that form a coherent basin within S̃."* This is admissible. It relates symbols to symbols.

**Generative "why"** — *"Why does matter have mass at all?"* → This requests access to ℐ — the pre-symbolic origin of the mass phenomenon. The question cannot be answered from within S̃, because ℐ_mass is not accessible there. This is inadmissible.

**Proposition:** Generative "why" questions are inadmissible within a measurement-grounded symbolic system due to the non-invertibility of the generonic projection.

This is not ignorance. It is **structural constraint**. The architecture of symbolic systems makes the generative origin of symbols inaccessible from within those systems.

## 7. How This Differs from Gödel

The essay explicitly compares this limit to Gödel's:

| | Gödel (ATT_36) | Generonic Boundary (ATT_37) |
|--|----------------|------------------------------|
| **What is limited** | Provability within the system | Formulability at the boundary of the system |
| **Location of limit** | Inside S̃ — within the symbolic system | At the edge of S̃ — between S̃ and ℐ |
| **Type of limit** | Symbolic insufficiency: there are truths the system cannot prove | Epistemic grounding: there are questions the system cannot meaningfully ask |
| **What exists beyond** | True but unprovable statements | The interaction domain ℐ — prior to symbolisation |
| **Result** | Indeterminate statements | Inadmissible questions |

Gödel's incompleteness is an **internal** limit — the system is rich enough to generate questions it cannot answer. The Generonic Boundary is a **boundary** limit — the system cannot even formulate questions about what lies prior to its own symbol formation.

Gödel pointed inward. The Generonic Boundary points outward — or rather, *prior*.

Wittgenstein is also relevant: questions outside language-games lose their meaning rather than their answer. The Generonic Boundary gives this intuition a formal grounding: questions about ℐ are not merely outside our language-game; they are outside the structure of symbol formation itself.

## 8. "Why" Reframed, Not Rejected

**The value of "why" is not diminished. It is reframed.**

In scientific practice, "why" functions as a **heuristic** for:
- Identifying relationships between variables within S̃ (relational "why")
- Constructing predictive models that stabilise within shared symbolic frameworks
- Building consensus — communities stabilise around shared explanatory structures

The recursive "why" of a child — "but why? but why? but why?" — is **boundary-probing dynamics**. The chain explores the structure of S̃, pushing toward its edges. It does not terminate in generative origin (it cannot — origin lies in ℐ). It terminates when the chain either:
- Reaches a basin-stable symbolic framework (consensus)
- Reaches the Generonic Boundary (inadmissibility)

Both are legitimate outcomes. The child is not doing something wrong. They are exploring the structure of the admissible symbolic space — and occasionally, without knowing it, touching the boundary.

---

## 9. Failure Modes: Structural Misalignment

Section 8 of the essay identifies a pattern in contemporary theoretical work:

**Structural misalignment** occurs when a symbolic system presents itself as accounting for ℐ while remaining within S̃. Examples:

| Theoretical move | The misalignment |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| Introduction of unobservable dimensions (extra dimensions in string theory) | Symbolic extensions within S̃ presented as descriptions of ℐ |
| Reification of model-layer constructs (treating fields or wavefunctions as ontologically primary) | Symbols mistaken for the interactions that produced them |
| Generative origin claims in theoretical physics (e.g., "why is there something rather than nothing?") | Requests for ℐ-access from within S̃ |

These approaches are not necessarily wrong in their predictions — they may be **useful fictions** (Pillar 4) with genuine empirical success within their designed regime. But they are **misleading in their explanatory claims** when they present themselves as having inverted the Generonic Boundary.

The FSM criterion: a theory is structurally aligned if its claims remain within S̃ (as relational statements) and it does not represent itself as accessing ℐ.
