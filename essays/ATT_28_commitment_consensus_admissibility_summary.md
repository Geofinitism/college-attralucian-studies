## Core trajectory

Before any formal mathematical system can operate, there must already exist an implicit agreement about what entities are admissible, what operations are permitted, and what forms of reasoning are valid. These agreements are not derived from logic — they precede it. Logic itself presupposes a set of admissible objects and relations; it cannot, by itself, determine their existence.

This essay names, defines, and traces the consequences of a three-part structure underlying all mathematical systems: **commitment**, **consensus**, and **admissibility**. Commitment is the initial act — the selection of primitives and assumptions that define the landscape of discourse. Consensus is the process by which repeated use transforms contingent commitments into apparent necessities, eventually making alternative commitments unthinkable rather than merely unchosen. Admissibility is the deepest layer: it defines what may count as a legitimate mathematical object, operation, or result, and it is prior to logic rather than derived from it.

The essay then traces the institutional mechanisms by which these three elements are maintained: **curation** (the distributed filtering process of journals, peer review, editorial boards, and informal networks of influence); **access** (the conditions of participation that shape who may enter the processes through which consensus is formed); and **momentum** (the accumulated weight of foundational documents, canonical problems, and shared narratives — the *mathematical mythos* that carries the discipline's identity across generations).

Together, these processes produce a historically asymmetric structure: early in a discipline's development, admissibility is porous and multiple commitments may compete; as the discipline matures, the threshold for entry rises steeply. The result is that innovation becomes increasingly localised within existing frameworks, while more fundamental shifts face barriers that are not logical in nature — they are institutional, narrative, and structural.

The essay's final movement positions the **outsider** as a structural role in the development of mathematics. Following Kuhn's observation that significant perspective shifts often arise from positions not fully embedded in prevailing paradigms, the essay argues that proposals grounded in different admissibility criteria cannot be evaluated solely by reference to the internal criteria of the existing system: to do so would presuppose the universality of those criteria, excluding alternative foundations by definition.

**Finite Symbolic Mechanics (FSM)** — the Geofinite approach — is positioned explicitly as one such alternative admissibility domain: grounded in finite, measurable interactions, treating uncertainty as intrinsic, rejecting reliance on unmeasurable constructs. It does not negate classical mathematics; it re-contextualises it as an internally consistent **endogenous** system — valid within its domain, but not constrained by direct correspondence to measurement. FSM, by contrast, proposes an admissibility framework in which such correspondence is **primary**. The two systems coexist, separated by a **separatrix** — a structural boundary arising from differing foundational commitments, not a hierarchy of correctness.

## Key claim

**Admissibility is prior to logic.** Mathematical systems do not arise from logic alone — they arise through a triadic process in which pre-logical conditions determine the domain within which logic operates. The apparent necessity of mathematical results is conditional upon accepted admissibility criteria, not absolute. The absence of a unified mathematical canon is not accidental but structural — a measurable consequence of the fact that no universal admissibility agreement exists. Classical mathematics and Geofinitism (FSM) are both valid within their respective admissibility domains, separated by a separatrix rather than ranked in a hierarchy of correctness.

## The Triadic Structure (figure 1)

Admissibility is the foundation. Above it, Commitment selects specific primitives and assumptions. Above that, Consensus reinforces and narrows admissibility over time.

The triangle is not a hierarchy of importance but a hierarchy of precedence: admissibility is prior, commitment is explicit, consensus is the emergent stabilisation of both.

## The Machinery of Stabilisation

Three mechanisms maintain the triad in a mature mathematical discipline:

**Curation:** Journals, editorial boards, peer review, and informal networks of influence function as implicit admissibility filters. Submission to a journal is not merely presenting a result — it is positioning within an existing admissibility domain. Work not recognisable as meaningful within the prevailing framework may be regarded as unclear or unmotivated, even if internally coherent.

**Access:** Participation in mathematical development requires alignment with established methods, domain-specific language, and recognition by field members. These conditions are not formalised but are nonetheless operative. The growth of specialisation and institutional density has stratified access further: the contemporary mathematical landscape is characterised by scale, specialisation, and layered access.

**Momentum / Mathematical Mythos:** Foundational documents, canonical problems, and shared narratives acquire status extending beyond their technical content. These become reference points within a broader story of the discipline's identity. Early stabilisations carry forward as enduring structures, reducing the need to revisit foundational commitments at each stage — but introducing inertia against fundamental shifts.

## The Admissibility Asymmetry (figure 2)

In the **formative phase**: admissibility boundaries are porous; multiple commitments may compete; the threshold for entry is low.

In the **mature phase**: accumulated momentum thickens the boundary; the threshold for new foundational commitments rises steeply; innovation becomes increasingly localised within existing frameworks.

**Key consequence:** Ideas that do not map onto existing admissibility domains may encounter difficulty not due to internal inconsistency but due to their distance from the prevailing framework. Without initial acceptance, there can be no curation; without curation, no consensus; without consensus, no stabilisation.

## Classical Mathematics and FSM as Coexistent Domains (figure 3)

The essay's most significant positioning statement: classical mathematics and Finite Symbolic Mechanics are not in conflict — they are **separated by a separatrix**, two regions of conceptual space defined by different foundational commitments.

| Classical mathematics | Finite Symbolic Mechanics |
|----------------------|--------------------------|
| Endogenous — internally consistent, self-validating | Exogenous — grounded in measurable interactions |
| Admissibility not constrained by measurement | Admissibility requires measurement correspondence |
| Accepts real continuum, infinite sets, limits | Rejects unmeasurable constructs; finitude is primary |
| Uncertainty as limiting case or absence of knowledge | Uncertainty as intrinsic |

FSM does not negate classical mathematics. It re-contextualises it as one internally coherent realisation among many, defined by a particular set of historical commitments. Classical mathematics is valid within its admissibility domain; FSM is valid within its own.

**The separatrix is structural, not evaluative.** It does not indicate which domain is correct — both are internally coherent — but where their foundational commitments diverge.

## The Absence of a Unified Canon

A purely logical enterprise grounded in fixed universal primitives would produce a single canonical "book of proofs." **No such canon exists.** This is not historical accident — it is structural. A universal canon would require prior global agreement on admissibility, which does not exist. Different areas of mathematics operate under different implicit commitments, stabilised through local consensus rather than universal adoption.

The absence of a unified canon is thus a **measurable exogenous observation** — evidence that mathematics is not unified at the level of logic alone, but is conditioned by pre-logical factors.
