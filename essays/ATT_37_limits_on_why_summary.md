## Core trajectory

The essay opens with a diagnostic: the question "why" has long been regarded as the highest form of explanation — in Aristotle, in modern physics, in everyday inquiry. But its legitimacy as a universal question has rarely been examined in relation to *the conditions under which symbols themselves are formed*. That examination reveals a structural boundary.

### The Setup: Measurement is Primary

Finite Symbolic Mechanics (FSM) begins with a commitment: measurement is primary. Symbols are not arbitrary abstractions — they arise from finite, resolution-bounded interactions. Symbol formation involves three requirements:

1. A bounded capture of interaction
2. A minimum cost of distinction ΔM
3. Compression into a finite symbolic domain

Two domains are distinguished:

| Domain | Notation | Status | Access |
|--------|----------|--------|--------|
| Interaction domain | ℐ | Inadmissible | Cannot be directly represented |
| Symbolic domain | S̃ | Admissible | All scientific models operate here |

Symbol formation is a **non-invertible projection**:

$$\tilde{\mathcal{G}} : \mathcal{I} \rightarrow \tilde{\mathcal{S}}$$

The tilde marks S̃ as a finite symbolic construction, not a classical mathematical space. Information not captured within the generonic event is not represented in S̃ and cannot be recovered.

### The Generonic Boundary

**Definition:** The Generonic Boundary is the interface at which interaction is projected into symbolic form under finite constraint.

This boundary separates ℐ (inadmissible) from S̃ (admissible). There exists no admissible operation that maps from S̃ back to ℐ. All scientific models, all theorems, all explanations — they operate within S̃. The boundary is a one-way gate.

### The Structure of "Why"

To ask "why does X occur?" is to request a generative account of X. Formally, this requires identifying ℐ_X ∈ ℐ such that:

$$\tilde{\mathcal{G}}(\mathcal{I}_X) = \tilde{X}$$

But ℐ_X is not accessible within S̃. Any admissible answer must instead take the form of a relation within the symbolic domain:

$$\tilde{X} \sim f(\tilde{Y}_1, \tilde{Y}_2, \ldots)$$

This produces a fundamental bifurcation:

1. The "why" reduces to a **relational explanation within S̃** — admissible
2. The "why" refers to ℐ and becomes **inadmissible**

**Proposition:** Generative "why" questions are inadmissible within a measurement-grounded symbolic system due to the non-invertibility of the generonic projection.

This is not ignorance — it is structural constraint.

### Historical Situating

Three antecedents are examined:

**Aristotle:** Four causes (material, formal, efficient, final) — a taxonomy of explanation. Scientific thought narrowed this toward efficient/mechanistic causation, culminating in classical physics. But even Aristotle's causal analysis operates entirely within what FSM would call S̃.

**Gödel:** Incompleteness theorems reveal limits *internal* to formal systems — unprovable truths that exist within a system but cannot be reached by that system's own proof apparatus.

**Wittgenstein:** Questions outside the structure of language-games lose their *meaning* rather than their *answer*. The present work is closer to Wittgenstein than to Gödel — but with a formal grounding that Wittgenstein's language-game analysis did not provide.

**The precise distinction:**

| Thinker | Limit identified | Type of limit |
|---------|-----------------|---------------|
| Gödel | Unprovable truths within the system | Symbolic insufficiency (internal) |
| Wittgenstein | Meaningless questions outside language-games | Grammatical/use-based |
| Generonic Boundary | Unaskable questions at the boundary of the system | Epistemic grounding (the pre-symbolic) |

Gödel's limit is *inside* S̃. The Generonic Boundary limit is *at the edge* of S̃ — it concerns what cannot be referenced because it lies prior to symbol formation itself.

---

### "Why" in Practice: Reframed, Not Rejected

Despite its structural inadmissibility in the generative sense, "why" remains indispensable as a **heuristic**:

- Identifying relationships between variables (relational "why")
- Constructing predictive models (structural "why")
- Stabilising understanding within a community (social/consensus "why")

The recursive "why" phase in childhood development represents **boundary-probing dynamics** — an exploration of explanatory structure. These chains do not terminate in generative origin (they cannot — origin lies in ℐ). They stabilise within shared symbolic frameworks.

The value of "why" is not diminished. It is reframed: "why" is the engine of inquiry within S̃, not a route to ℐ.

---

### Failure Modes in Contemporary Theory

Section 8 identifies a recurring pattern: symbolic systems extended beyond admissibility. Examples include:

- Introduction of unobservable dimensions (extra dimensions in string theory, dark energy fields)
- Reification of model-layer constructs (treating mathematical objects as physical entities)
- Attempts to explain generative origin using symbolic extensions

Such approaches **remain within S̃ while presenting themselves as accounts of ℐ**. They constitute attempts to invert the generonic projection. They are structurally misaligned — not necessarily wrong in their predictions (they may be useful fictions, valid within their domain), but misleading in their explanatory claims.

### Conclusion

> "Explanation remains possible, but only within the symbolic domain from which it arises."

The Generonic Boundary provides a formal account of where explanation must stop — not because of ignorance, but because of structure. It identifies the point at which "why" ceases to be a question and becomes an attempt to invert a non-invertible map.

## Key claim

**Within a measurement-grounded symbolic system (Finite Symbolic Mechanics), the question "why" bifurcates: either it reduces to a relational statement within the symbolic domain S̃ (admissible) or it requests the generative origin of a symbol by inverting the Generonic Boundary G̃: ℐ → S̃ (inadmissible, because the projection is non-invertible). The interaction domain ℐ is structurally prior to S̃ and cannot be accessed from within it. This is not Gödel's limit (unprovable truths inside the system) but a distinct limit: unformulable questions at the boundary of the system. "Why" retains full heuristic value as the engine of relational inquiry; it loses admissibility only when interpreted as a demand for generative origin. The Generonic Boundary formally delimits this loss.**

## Ten Open Questions (Section 10)

The essay closes with a genuine research programme:

1. Can all scientific explanations be reformulated purely as relational statements within S̃?
2. What criteria distinguish admissible symbolic extension from inadmissible generative claims?
3. How does the Generonic Boundary relate to existing notions of observability and operationalism in physics?
4. Are there formal correspondences between the Generonic Boundary and limits in computation or information theory?
5. To what extent can developmental "why" questioning be modelled as boundary-probing dynamics?
6. Does recognising unaskable questions alter the methodology of scientific theory construction?
7. Can this framework be experimentally constrained through analysis of representational cost (Nexil chains, ΔM)?
