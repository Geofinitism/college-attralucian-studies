## Core trajectory

The essay opens with a compression claim that frames everything which follows: **all finite symbolic construction is necessarily compressive**. A system cannot preserve the full structure of interaction; it must stabilise it into a finite symbolic form under constraint. What the earlier Finite Mechanics programme called "generon cost" or "ink" is not an additional property imposed on the system — it is the direct consequence of this compression requirement. Geometry is itself an expression of compression. Distance, scale, and redshift are features arising from how extended interaction is compressed into finite symbolic form.

### Section 1 — Measurement as Interaction

All observations are local. A detector registers discrete interaction events arising from electromagnetic processes — finite, noisy, incomplete. A spectral measurement is therefore not the direct observation of a distant object or propagating entity, but a structured record of local interaction:

$$x(t) = \text{detected signal at time } t$$

**The photon reinterpreted:** The photon is understood as a symbolic construct — a generonic stabilisation of repeated interaction patterns — rather than a directly observed entity. The photon is not the thing that travels; it is the name given to the stable form that the interaction takes when successfully reconstructed.

### Section 2 — Spectra as Time Series

A spectrum (intensity as a function of wavelength) is in practice obtained through temporal integration of interaction events. Each spectral measurement is a time series vector:

$$S(t) = [I(\lambda_1), I(\lambda_2), \dots, I(\lambda_n)]$$

Successive measurements form the sequence: S(t₁), S(t₂), S(t₃), ...

The spectral line associated with a given atomic transition is therefore not a fixed quantity but a temporally evolving signal. The static spectrum of classical physics is an abstraction; the underlying reality is temporal.

### Section 3 — Embedding and Reconstruction

Using Takens' Theorem, the underlying dynamical structure of the interaction is reconstructed from a single observable:

$$\mathbf{X}(t) = (x(t),\; x(t+\tau))$$

This embedding maps the time series into a geometric trajectory in a reconstructed phase space. For coherent signals, this trajectory forms a closed or quasi-closed structure around a stable attractor. The attractor represents the persistent structure of the interaction process — not a static property of an external object.

**Key reinterpretation:** The "object" being observed is not a distant star or galaxy. It is the stable attractor formed by the local interaction sequence, reconstructed geometrically from the time series.

### Section 4 — Translation and Normalisation

A centering operation removes baseline offsets:

$$x'(t) = x(t) - \bar{x}$$

This removes absolute wavelength calibration offsets, allowing the intrinsic structure of the interaction to be examined. After centering, different sources yield trajectories of similar shape but differing scale. The shape is the structure; the scale is the cost.

### Section 5 — Redshift as Geometric Transformation

In conventional analysis, redshift is a scalar shift in wavelength. In the embedded framework, this shift manifests as a geometric transformation — a scaling of the reconstructed trajectory:

$$\mathbf{X}'(t) = \alpha \mathbf{X}(t)$$

**Redshift reinterpreted:** Redshift is a **radial expansion** in representational space, not a displacement of a spectral line and not the geometric expansion of Platonic spacetime. The parameter α encodes the scale difference between source and receiver — equivalently, the cost difference between sustaining the original interaction and reconstructing it locally.

### Section 6 — The Cost of Representation (Ink)

The stabilisation of a spectral signal requires interactional effort:
- Accumulation of detection events
- Temporal integration
- Noise reduction
- Calibration and processing

This effort is the **generon cost** (or *ink*):

$$C_{\text{gen}} = \text{cost of stabilising interaction into representation}$$

In embedded space, this cost is expressed geometrically — not as a scalar number stored separately, but as the shape of the trajectory itself.

### Section 7 — Radius and Circumference as Cost

The radius of the reconstructed trajectory reflects the scale of the signal. The circumference represents the total extent of stabilisation:

$$\text{Circumference} \sim 2\pi r$$

The circumference is the total representational effort required to sustain the interaction as a stable form. Therefore:

$$d \sim C_{\text{gen}} \sim \text{Circumference}$$

**Distance is not directly measured.** It is expressed as the cost of representation — the circumference of the Alphonic container that holds the reconstructed trajectory. Near sources require small containers; distant sources require large containers.

### Section 8 — Discrete Alphonic Boundary

The reconstructed trajectory appears continuous but is assembled from discrete interaction events. The boundary is therefore discrete:

$$\text{Circumference} \approx N \cdot \varepsilon$$

where N is the number of stabilised interaction events and ε is the minimum Alphonic unit — the smallest bead of ink through which a symbol can be formed.

**The Alphonic Limit:** ε defines the smallest distinguishable increment along the boundary and constrains how finely a trajectory can be resolved. Redshift is not realised as a continuous transformation but as a **reconfiguration of discrete stabilisations**. The measurable shift is shaped not only by the underlying interaction but by the finite granularity of representation itself.

This is the spectroscopic expression of Pillar 5: there is a floor below which the measurement cannot go, and that floor is the Alphonic unit ε.

### Section 9 — The Alphon as Representational Container

The embedded trajectory defines a bounded region within which the interaction is stabilised. This region is identified as an **Alphon** — a finite container of representation, within which meaning is maintained.

The Alphon's boundary is traced by the reconstructed trajectory. Its size reflects the cost required to hold the interaction in stable form:

| Source | Alphon size | Generon cost |
|--------|------------|-------------|
| Near-field | Small Alphon | Low cost |
| Distant observation | Large Alphon | Greater stabilisation effort |

The Alphon is not imposed on the measurement — it is what the measurement is. When we observe a spectrum, we are measuring the boundary of an Alphonic container.

### Sections 10–12 — Discussion, Conclusion, Closing Reflection

The framework replaces five classical assumptions:

| Classical reading | Geofinite reading |
|-------------------|------------------|
| Object ontology | Interaction as primary element |
| Static spectral properties | Time series of interaction events |
| Direct observation | Embedding and reconstruction |
| Scalar redshift | Geometric transformation α in representational space |
| Abstract distance | Cost C_gen ~ Circumference ~ N·ε |

**Conclusion:** Spectral measurement is not the observation of distant objects but the stabilisation of local interactions into persistent forms. Through embedding, these forms reveal underlying dynamical structure. The effort required to sustain this structure — generon cost — defines the scale of representation. Distance is not a directly measured quantity but an emergent property of the interactional cost required to maintain a stable representation within an Alphonic container.

**Closing reflection:** "What is taken to be distant is never directly encountered. It is held — step by step — through the accumulation of finite acts of stabilisation... distance is not traversed but constructed. The trajectory closes. The boundary stabilises. The representation holds — for a time. And in that holding, the cost is paid."
