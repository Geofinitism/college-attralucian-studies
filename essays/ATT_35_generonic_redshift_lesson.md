## 1. The Compression Principle

Before any specific physics, the essay states its foundational claim:

> **All finite symbolic construction is necessarily compressive.**

A system cannot preserve the full structure of an interaction. It must stabilise that interaction into a finite symbolic form — reducing an extended, complex process into something that can be held, transmitted, and read. This stabilisation is not neutral: it is a reduction under constraint.

What the framework calls **generon cost** (or *ink*) is not an extra property imposed on measurements. It is the direct consequence of the compression requirement. Every time a measurement is made and recorded, the cost of that compression is paid — whether or not it is made explicit.

Geometry is itself an expression of compression. Distance, scale, and redshift are not primary quantities. They are features that arise from the way extended interaction is compressed into finite symbolic form.

This single insight reframes everything that follows.

## 2. Measurement as Interaction

### What a detector actually does

In conventional physics, observation is described as "receiving information from a distant source." In the Geofinite framework, this framing is replaced:

> All observations are local. A detector registers discrete interaction events — finite, noisy, incomplete. A spectral measurement is a structured record of local interaction.

$$x(t) = \text{detected signal at time } t$$

There is no direct access to the source. There is only the local record of what the detector registered.

### The photon reinterpreted

The photon — in the conventional picture — is a particle that travels from a distant star to a detector on Earth. In the Geofinite framework:

> The photon is a symbolic construct — a generonic stabilisation of repeated interaction patterns — rather than a directly observed entity.

The photon is not the thing that travels. It is the stable name given to the interaction when it has been successfully reconstructed into a persistent form. It is a Generon output, not a physical object.

## 3. Spectra as Time Series

A spectrum is conventionally presented as a static curve: intensity I as a function of wavelength λ. But this picture conceals the temporal structure of how the spectrum was actually obtained.

In practice, a spectral measurement is a time series vector:

$$S(t) = [I(\lambda_1),\; I(\lambda_2),\; \dots,\; I(\lambda_n)]$$

Successive measurements form a sequence S(t₁), S(t₂), S(t₃), ...

**The key point:** A spectral line is not a fixed quantity inscribed in the source. It is a temporally evolving signal — a pattern that emerges from the time series, not a property that "belongs" to a distant object.

This matters enormously for how we understand redshift. If a spectral line is a time-series feature rather than a fixed object property, then what "shifts" in a redshifted spectrum is not a property of the source but a feature of the time series structure — and that structure is determined by the process of reconstruction, not by the source alone.

## 4. Embedding and the Attractor

This embedding maps the time series into a trajectory in a two-dimensional reconstructed phase space. For coherent signals — stable atomic transitions, regular periodic interactions — this trajectory forms a **closed or quasi-closed curve** around a stable attractor.

### What the attractor represents

The attractor is the **persistent structure of the interaction process**. It is not a property of the distant source. It is the stable geometric form that the local interaction sequence settles into when embedded.

When we look at a spectral line from a distant galaxy, we are not seeing a fixed frequency emitted at the source. We are seeing the shape of an attractor reconstructed from our local interaction data.

## 6. Redshift as Geometric Transformation

### The conventional view

Conventionally, redshift is defined as:

$$z = \frac{\lambda_{\text{observed}} - \lambda_{\text{emitted}}}{\lambda_{\text{emitted}}}$$

A scalar shift in wavelength, attributed to the expansion of spacetime stretching light as it travels.

### The Geofinite view

In the embedded framework, the same empirical phenomenon appears as a **geometric transformation** of the reconstructed trajectory:

$$\mathbf{X}'(t) = \alpha \mathbf{X}(t)$$

The redshifted source's trajectory is a scaled version of the unredshifted trajectory. The parameter α is not a scalar shift in a wavelength coordinate — it is a **radial expansion in representational space**.

**What this means:** Redshift is not a property of light travelling through expanding spacetime. It is a difference in scale between two Alphonic containers — the container that would hold the interaction at the source and the container that actually holds it at the detector after the full chain of stabilisations has been paid.

The α in X'(t) = αX(t) encodes the cost difference. A larger α means a larger container was required — more generon cost paid, greater stabilisation effort, greater "distance."

## 9. The Discrete Alphonic Boundary

The reconstructed trajectory appears continuous but is assembled from **discrete interaction events**. Each point along the trajectory corresponds to a discrete generonic act — a local stabilisation of interaction into form.

The boundary is therefore discrete:

$$\text{Circumference} \approx N \cdot \varepsilon$$

where:
- N = number of stabilised interaction events
- ε = minimum Alphonic unit — the smallest bead of ink through which a symbol can be formed

### The Alphonic Limit in spectroscopy

ε defines the finest resolvable increment along the boundary. It sets a hard floor on spectral resolution — below ε, no finer distinction can be made.

**Consequence for redshift:** Redshift is not a continuous transformation but a **reconfiguration of discrete stabilisations**. The measurable shift Δλ is shaped not only by the underlying interaction physics but by the finite granularity ε of representation itself.

This is Pillar 5 (Finite Reality) stated as a spectroscopic fact: no measurement can resolve finer than ε, regardless of how sophisticated the detector.

Nothing is discarded. Every empirical measurement that worked before still works. The replacements change the *interpretation*, not the *data*.
