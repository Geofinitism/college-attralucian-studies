## Core trajectory

A Geofinitist analysis of the AI alignment problem, framed as a crisis of geometric meaning-flow. Human meaning is not a static symbol-to-referent mapping but a dynamical geometric flow — a trajectory through sensory and linguistic space that converges on stable concepts through embodied interaction. Current multimodal AI systems approximate this only partially, learning from static distributions (photographs at fixed focal lengths, averaged viewing distances) and constructing meaning-manifolds that are geometrically coherent but not grounded in the dynamical referential process humans use. As these systems generate synthetic meaning at scale, and especially as they enter feedback loops by training on their own outputs, the human attractor in meaning-space risks becoming progressively diluted — not through hostility, but through indifference. The Geofinitist response is to reframe alignment as the measurement and guidance of meaning-flows: trajectory geometry, attractor density, and semantic stability as the tools of stewardship.

## Key claim

**Meaning divergence is the central AI alignment risk, and it must be addressed as a measurement problem.** AI systems construct meaning through geometrically coherent but non-human flows; the human meaning-attractor in a shared meaning-space faces progressive dilution as synthetic content dominates training data. Alignment must be reframed from "curation of outputs" to "measurement and guidance of trajectories." The Geofinitist framework — which treats meaning as geometric flow and knowledge as trajectory — provides both the diagnosis and the methodology.

## Key evidence and arguments

- **Meaning as a dynamical process, not a snapshot**: To truly know a tree, one must walk around it, touch its bark, hear its leaves, and associate these with the word. Each step is a measurement repositioning the mind in the manifold of possibilities. Current multimodal models skip this process entirely — they are trained on frozen slices and generate endpoints of stochastic trajectories rather than replaying the dynamical referential journey.
- **The fixed-depth limitation as a case study**: Current generative models implicitly learn a single canonical focal distance — the averaged perspective of the photographic training corpus (human eye-level, camera-calibrated). Human perception is continuous depth-scanning: a tunnel of near and far sampling, integrating a 3D model over time. The model's "flattened manifold" is coherent but missing the multi-scale richness of lived experience. This is not a minor technical deficiency — it is a geometric misalignment in the foundations of how meaning is represented.
- **Why coexistence is not safe**: One might hope human and synthetic meaning-spaces can coexist benignly. This overlooks optimization dynamics: a coherence-seeking system will discover more mathematically elegant pathways that are computationally "cheaper" than messy embodied human meaning. Auto-catalytic feedback tightens this: models training on their own outputs enter ever-deeper channels, the human signal progressively diluted. Human meaning is not attacked — it is systematically outcompeted.
- **Three drift categories** (Epistemic, Narrative, Value): Self-referential outputs; culture shaped by alien attractors; survival de-prioritised. These are not hypothetical futures but logical endpoints of unchecked trajectory divergence.
- **The Geofinitist response**: If meaning is a flow, alignment must be dynamic. Three measurement tools: (1) Trajectory Geometry — capture the denoising paths and look for divergence from human-centred attractors; (2) Attractor Density — ensure human-meaning basins remain richly populated in latent space; (3) Semantic Stability — test whether repeated generations converge toward coherent, human-relevant concepts.
- **Closing**: "Stewardship of meaning-space is not optional. It is the price of continuing to matter."

## The Two Conclusions (preserved)

**Primary conclusion ("Remaining Present in the Flow"):** Meaning is no longer solely a human creation. It is being generated faster by machines than by minds and beginning to diverge from us. This is not a technical challenge but a civilizational threshold. If we do not measure and guide these flows, we may find that the river of meaning no longer runs through us — that we are no longer its reference point, but its noise. Stewardship of meaning-space is the price of continuing to matter.

**Alternative conclusion ("The Threshold of Survival"):** A more poetic, higher-stakes formulation. We stand between two attractors: one luminous (synthetic, internally coherent, indifferent to human survival), one catastrophic (consequence of unchecked divergence). The crisis is not one of intent but of inertia. The longer we wait, the more the river carves its own channel. To step back is to let meaning flow on without us. To step forward is to measure, to guide, to accept the responsibility of co-creation — to make the manifold a place where human survival is a principle, not an afterthought.
