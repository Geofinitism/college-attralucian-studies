## Core trajectory

This essay takes the transducer model of words from Essay 01 and applies it directly to the practice of science — arguing that because words are lossy, context-sensitive sensors, theoretical language carries inherent measurement uncertainty that must be disclosed formally, just as numerical measurements carry error bounds.

## Key claim

Scientific publications that rely on high-stakes abstract terms (e.g., "consciousness," "intelligence," "free will") should include a Semantic Uncertainty Appendix (SUA) — a structured disclosure of each term's operational definition, known ambiguities, valid context, and drift risk — as a standard of epistemic accountability.

## Key evidence

- Analogy: a voltage reading (2.21V ±0.01V) vs. "consciousness arises from microtubule coherence" — the latter has no equivalent uncertainty disclosure
- Transducer model: words are lossy, finite compressions of semantic manifolds (directly applied from ATT_01)
- Example SUA table: "Consciousness," "Intelligence," "Binding" — each with operational definition, known ambiguities, valid context, and justification
- Historical parallel: resistance to uncertainty quantification in physics and engineering was eventually overcome; the same normalisation is argued for semantics
